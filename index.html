Single-Baseline Polarimetric SAR Interferometry — Papathanassiou & Cloude (2001)

An interactive reconstruction of the paper's core contribution: given a random-volume-over-ground scene, the interferometric coherence γ(w) depends on the choice of polarization w only through the ground-to-volume amplitude ratio m(w). Since m(w) is a generalized Rayleigh quotient of the ground and volume Pauli coherency matrices, its extremal values are exactly a generalized eigenvalue problem — the origin of "polarimetric coherence optimization." The three optimum coherences all lie, by construction, on the RVoG line through γV.

Source: K.P. Papathanassiou, S.R. Cloude, "Single-Baseline Polarimetric SAR Interferometry," IEEE TGRS, vol. 39, no. 11, Nov. 2001 (PapCloude2001.pdf provided by the user).

1 — Coherence optimization: the eigenproblem, live

Solving TGw=m·TVw (a generalized Rayleigh-quotient eigenproblem, exact under the paper's reflection-symmetry ground model, Eqs.16-17) gives three ordered ground-to-volume ratios m1≥m2≥m3 and their scattering mechanisms w1,w2,w3. Feeding each mj into the scalar RVoG mixing formula (Eq.12) gives the three optimum coherences — which, as the paper's Eq.21/Fig.6 shows, must lie exactly on the straight line through γV in the direction (1−γV). 2 — Fig.6 spirit: phase-center separation vs. uncertainty

Effective phase-center height hj≈arg(γj)/κz for each optimum channel, ±1σ from N-look phase noise. Good conditioning (Section IV-A) needs the three hj separated by more than their combined uncertainty — high coherence alone is not enough.

3 — "Ground varies ~25 dB with polarization, volume only ~5-10 dB"

Diagonal (co-pol/cross-pol channel) intensities of TV and TG in dB, illustrating the paper's key qualitative claim about why ground scattering — not volume — drives the polarization-dependence of PolInSAR coherence.

4 — Fig.9 spirit: why a wide m-spectrum conditions better

The paper's own two scenarios (m1:m2:m3 = 1.0:0.1:0.01 "wide" vs. 0.1:0.05:0.01 "narrow") applied to the Section IV-A conditioning criterion directly: each optimum channel's effective phase-center height ± its N-look uncertainty, across a true-height sweep (5-35 m). Left (wide): the three hj stay clearly separated at every height. Right (narrow): h2 and h3 stay close together and their error bars often overlap — the geometric reason a narrow spectrum makes the joint inversion ill-conditioned, independent of any specific solver.

TV=mV·diag(1,f,f) (Eq.16); TG=[[a,c,0],[c*,b,0],[0,0,d]]·m̄G (Eq.17, reflection symmetry ⇒ block-diagonal, c=ρ√(ab) here). Because both matrices share this block structure, the generalized eigenproblem TGw=m·TVw decomposes exactly into one 2×2 analytic problem (Pauli channels 1-2) plus one decoupled 1×1 (HV-like channel 3) — solved in closed form, no iterative eigensolver needed. γj=eiφ₀(γV+mje-2σh_V/cosθ₀)/(1+mje-2σh_V/cosθ₀), γV the standard RVoG volume coherence (same closed form used across this dashboard series).
