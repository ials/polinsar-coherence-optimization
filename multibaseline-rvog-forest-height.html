Multi-Baseline Forest Height Estimation — Analytic + Geometric RVoG (Zhang et al., 2024)

Illustrates the paper's core claims about the random-volume-over-ground (RVoG) model under a multi-baseline PolInSAR configuration: each interferometric baseline traces its own straight coherence locus in the unit circle (Eq. 1–4); real, noisy observations scatter around that line into an elliptical "coherence region" whose eccentricity indicates how trustworthy that baseline is (Eqs. 13–18); and because the ground-to-volume amplitude ratio can be normalized consistently across baselines (Eqs. 8–10), the other baselines can be converted into geometric constraints that are jointly minimized together with the reference baseline's analytic RVoG fit (Eqs. 19–29) — instead of throwing that information away, as "pick-one-optimal-baseline" methods do.

Source: B. Zhang, H. Zhu, W. Song, J. Zhu, J. Dai, J. Zhang, C. Li, "A Multi-Baseline Forest Height Estimation Method Combining Analytic and Geometric Expression of the RVoG Model," Forests 2024, 15, 1496 (forests-ruiz.pdf provided by the user).

1. RVoG coherence loci under a multi-baseline configuration (Fig. 1)

Each baseline's coherence locus is a straight line from eiφ0γV (pure volume, m=0) to eiφ0 (pure ground, m=∞); polarization diversity moves the observed point along that line (Eq. 4). Markers are simulated N-look noisy samples for several polarization channels per baseline; dashed lines are the theoretical loci. Different kz gives each baseline a different slope/position even though the scene (hV, σ, hg) is identical — the "ideal" (b) vs "real, noisy" (c) contrast from Figure 1.

2. Coherence-region eccentricity & baseline selection (Eqs. 13–18)

A flatter, more elongated ("linear") noisy point cloud → higher eccentricity → a baseline whose line-fit (and hence φ0, γV) can be trusted more. The paper selects the baseline of largest eccentricity as the reference baseline for the analytic RVoG fit; the rest become geometric-constraint baselines.

3. Single-baseline fit vs. added geometric constraints

Holding σ at its (assumed known) true value, sweeping a candidate forest height hV′ predicts a pure-volume point under each baseline's own kz (Eq. 2). "Reference baseline only" fits hV′ against all of that one baseline's own noisy polarization samples (Eq. 25/27/29, Q=1) — already fairly well-behaved once σ is known. Adding the perpendicular distance to a Cloude-style single extra baseline's line, or to every other baseline's line at once (Eq. 19–24, 29), further sharpens and stabilizes the minimum, especially at low look counts — try lowering "Looks N" and clicking "Resample noise" repeatedly to see the proposed (teal) curve track the true height more consistently than either single-baseline alternative.

What is exact vs. illustrative here

  • Exact, from the paper's equations: the RVoG volume decoherence γV(hV,σ,θ,kz) (Eq. 2); the coherence-line construction γ(w) = eiφ0(γV + L(w)(1−γV)) (Eq. 4); the reduction of per-baseline surface phase to one shared ground elevation hg via φ0q = kzqhg (Eq. 26); the perpendicular point-to-line distance used for the geometric constraint (Eq. 20/23); and the RMSE/R² numbers in panel 5, taken directly from the paper's Figures 8 and 10.
  • Faithful simplification: the paper's eccentricity criterion (Eqs. 13–18) is computed from a Schur decomposition of a 6×6 polarimetric coherency matrix Π built from all polarization channels at once. This dashboard instead simulates N-look noisy samples at a handful of representative polarization points per baseline and computes the eccentricity of their 2D (Re, Im) covariance ellipse — the same geometric idea (how elongated/linear the coherence region is), computed from simulated samples rather than a real polarimetric covariance matrix.
  • Simplified for interactivity: panel 3 fixes the extinction σ at its true/assumed value and sweeps only hV, whereas the paper's actual algorithm jointly solves for both hV and σ (plus the reference baseline's normalized ground-to-volume ratio) by nonlinear least squares (Eq. 28–29). Fixing σ isolates and visualizes the cross-baseline geometric-consistency mechanism (why multiple kz values pin down height better) without requiring a full 2-D nonlinear solver in the browser.
  • Illustrative, not the paper's real imagery: panels 1–4 use a synthetic scene and simulated N-look coherence noise (same complex-Gaussian sample-coherence model used in the other PolInSAR dashboards in this series) — the paper's real AfriSAR/Krycklan PolInSAR datasets are not redistributed here. Only panel 5's bars are the paper's own reported numbers.
