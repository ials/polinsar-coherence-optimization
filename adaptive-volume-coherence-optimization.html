Adaptive Volume Coherence Optimization (Ada-VolOpt) — Wan, Wang et al. (2026)

An interactive reconstruction of the paper's core pipeline for forest height inversion from single-polarization L-band bistatic InSAR (LuTan-1). Because a single polarization gives only one complex coherence per pixel, the observation space is first expanded by splitting the full-resolution SLC into several azimuth sub-look images (time-frequency analysis), each seeing a slightly different ground-to-volume scattering ratio. The sub-look coherences trace a line inside the complex unit circle (CUC) — the RVoG coherence line — whose intersection with the CUC gives the ground phase, and whose position defines a 1-D ambiguity interval for the volume-only coherence γv0. Ada-VolOpt resolves that ambiguity by optimizing γv0 against all available sub-look observations, then a slope-adaptive RVoG model inverts forest height while correcting for the terrain-slope bias that would otherwise distort κz and the local incidence angle.

Source: J. Wan, C. Wang, Y. Lei, E. Chen, A. Fu, Y. Liu, L. Zhao, X. Liu, J. Xu, Y. Fan, Q. Song, "Adaptive volume coherence optimization for forest height inversion in mountainous areas using innovative spaceborne L-band bistatic InSAR," GIScience & Remote Sensing, 63:1, 2637240 (2026). 1 · Expanding single-polarization InSAR: azimuth sub-look splitting

The full Doppler spectrum of the single-polarization SLC is split into N overlapping sub-looks, each seeing the forest from a slightly different azimuth observation angle. A narrower per-look bandwidth means less overlap (more distinct azimuth angles, more coherence dispersion) but a noisier, lower-resolution sub-look image.

2 · Sub-look coherences in the complex unit circle

Each sub-look coherence γ(az) lies (in theory) on the straight RVoG line through γv0 and the ground phase point eiφ₀. The line is fit by total least squares; extending it to the CUC gives two candidate ground-phase points. Coordinates below are de-rotated by the fitted ground phase (a standard normalization) so the chosen ground point always sits at exactly (1, 0) and γv0 lies directly on the line — γmin (least ground scattering) and γmax (most ground scattering) mark the observed extremes.

3 · Ada-VolOpt: resolving the 1-D ambiguity

Along the ambiguity segment between γmin and the line's far CUC intersection γe, every candidate γv0 is, by the line's own geometry, exactly self-consistent with any single sub-look taken alone — that is the ambiguity. What actually constrains it is that every sub-look's implied ground-to-volume ratio L(azi) must be a physically valid fraction in [0, 1] simultaneously; Ada-VolOpt takes the midpoint of the segment where that holds for all available sub-looks (shrinking toward the true value as noise drops or more/wider-spread sub-looks are added).

4 · Slope-adaptive forest height inversion (2-D lookup)

Forest height and extinction are jointly inverted by grid-searching the RVoG model for the (hV, σ) pair whose predicted volume coherence best matches the Ada-VolOpt estimate γ̂v0. When the slope-adaptive correction is switched off, the lookup model uses the nominal θ₀ and κz even though the true scene sits on a slope — reproducing the paper's terrain bias.

5 · Validation across three test sites (reported results)

Real accuracy figures reported in the paper for the Hainan (tropical), Yanbian (mixed), and Genhe (boreal) test sites, covering 63.11 thousand km² total, validated against airborne LiDAR (ERH100). The "ignoring ground scattering" bars are derived from the paper's stated relative accuracy improvements (8% / 10% / 33%) applied to the Ada-VolOpt accuracy — the paper does not tabulate the baseline RMSE directly.

6 · Sensitivity to Doppler bandwidth and number of sub-looks (illustrative)

The paper reports (Section 5.4, Figure 20) that a Doppler bandwidth of 60–70% and roughly five sub-look interferograms give the best accuracy/robustness trade-off, with boreal forests most sensitive to both due to low stand density. Illustrative simplifications: the paper does not give a closed-form relationship between azimuth sub-look angle and the ground-to-volume ratio L(az) — it is an empirical observation from real SAR data (Figures 1, 8) — so this dashboard uses a tunable synthetic az-dependence (with mild curvature, since a perfectly linear L(az) would make every sub-look redundant) to reproduce the qualitative CUC dispersion and bandwidth trade-off. The paper's own optimization residual (Eq. 9–12) could not be recovered exactly from the extracted PDF text (the multi-line equations render out of order under text extraction), so this had to be reconstructed from the description alone. Back-solving each sub-look's L from a *single* candidate γv0 is always exactly self-consistent for any point on the fitted line (a projective-line identity) — that is precisely the ambiguity, and no amount of azimuth curve-fitting from a single sub-look's own data can break it, whether by linear regression or by interpolating between only the two extremes (both are degenerate in the same way). What the paper's text does support directly ("traversing... within the bounds of Lmax and Lmin") is the one constraint that genuinely depends on the candidate: every sub-look's implied L must lie in the physical range [0, 1] simultaneously. Panel 3 takes the midpoint of the segment where that holds for every observed sub-look — a well-motivated stand-in for the paper's exact tie-breaking residual, which narrows toward the true γv0 as noise drops and more/wider-spread sub-looks are used, without pretending to reproduce a specific formula this dashboard could not verify. The correct-vs-ambiguous ground-phase decision (normally resolved with Kugler et al. 2015's rules) is simplified here to "closest to the known true topographic phase," for clarity. Panel 6's curves are qualitative illustrations of the described trends, not digitized Figure 20 data.
